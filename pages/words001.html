DRAFT

Software development is a loser's game

Riffing on Tyler Hawkins and Ben Hosking.

There's this observation from Simon Ramo about amateur tennis: nobody wins the match. Somebody loses it. Ball goes in the net, serve goes wide, someone trips over their own feet. The other person just... keeps it in play. That's it. That's the whole strategy.

Software's the same. I've been at this long enough to know the projects that go wrong aren't missing some flash of brilliance. They're drowning in their own daft mistakes. A misread requirement here. A test nobody wrote there. A merge on a Friday because someone couldn't be arsed waiting. A thousand tiny "it'll be reet"s piled up until the whole thing topples over.

The bit that sticks

What I like about this framing is how dead boring the winning strategy is. You don't need to be clever. You need to be careful. Read the diff. Run the tests. Ask the dumb question in the meeting. Check your assumptions. Nobody's writing blog posts about it, but it's the thing that actually matters.

Both articles cover the obvious stuff: jumping into code before you've understood the problem, not testing your own work, ignoring the standards your team agreed on last month. Aye, guilty on all counts at various points. I reckon everyone is if they're being honest. The interesting bit isn't spotting the mistakes though, it's building habits and systems that make them less likely.

Tooling isn't bureaucracy

Linters. Formatters. CI. Self-review checklists. People moan about these like they're overhead. They're not. They're the net that catches the ball before it hits the floor. I've worked on codebases with proper automated checks and codebases without. The difference is massive.

The tooling doesn't make you good. It stops you being rubbish on a Thursday afternoon when you're knackered and thinking about tea.

AI and LLMs fit in here too. Not as some replacement for thinking, but as another layer of "have I missed owt?" A second pair of eyes that doesn't get bored. It'll catch the typo in your SQL, point out you forgot to handle an error, or flag that your test doesn't actually assert anything useful. But when you let an LLM write code for you, you still own every line of it. Completely. If it introduces a bug, that's your bug now. If it picks a bad abstraction, you chose to keep it. Folk merge generated code without reading it properly because it "looks right" and came from a tool they trust. That's just a new flavour of the same unforced error. The tool wrote it. You shipped it.

The team angle

I think the original articles don't go far enough. They frame it as an individual thing, that you should be more careful. Right, fine. But how do you build a team that structurally makes fewer mistakes? That's the more interesting problem.

  • Small changes. Smaller diffs are easier to review. Easier to revert. Easier to reason about. This isn't controversial. Do it anyway.
  • Clear ownership. "Everyone owns it" means nobody does. Put a name on it.
  • Blameless post-mortems. You learn from mistakes you can talk about. If people get bollocked for errors, they hide them. Then you get bigger errors.
  • Make the right thing easy. If your test suite takes 40 minutes, people skip it. If deploys are scary, people batch them up. Fix the infrastructure. The humans will follow.

You don't win at software by being brilliant. You win by not being daft, or at least by being daft less often and catching it sooner.
