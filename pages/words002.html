The Uncanny Valley

I've been building this website recently, mainly as an experiment, mainly to see how to get my thoughts from my mind on to paper - screen if you will. Pair programming, trying to remember how things fitted together. Me thinking deeply, writing paragraphs of specs, about how things should work. I'm typing, giving it guidance and it's coming up with solutions. It's not perfect, so I have to jump in and write the correct solution or really tell it to think really really hard. And the thing that keeps striking me isn't how clever it is. It's how close it gets without quite landing.

See, I'm nostalgic for the old web. Not in a "things were better back then" way - well, maybe a bit - but that era when you'd view source on someone's site and it'd be hand-rolled HTML with inline styles and a visitor counter that didn't work. Geocities. Angelfire. Tables for layout. The whole lot. It were rough as owt but it were yours.

So I'm sat here asking an AI to help me recreate that feeling. An old time aesthetic. Grey panels. Bevelled edges. Sans-serif everything. And it does it. It absolutely does it. The CSS lands, the grab handles work, the little minimize buttons sit in the right spot. But there's this gap. Like someone's described your nan's kitchen from a photo and built it in a showroom. Looks right. Doesn't smell of toast.

The thing I'm chasing doesn't exist anymore. Maybe it never did. I remember the feeling of those old interfaces more than the reality. The reality was everything crashed, nothing worked on the first try, and you'd spend three hours debugging a floating div in IE6. But my brain's filed all that under "character" and kept the warm glow.

When an LLM recreates it, it gives you the version you described, not the version you remember. And those are different things. You describe the aesthetics but the memory includes the frustration, the discovery, the staying up til 2am because you finally got the DHTML dropdown menu to work and you felt like a god.

It's the uncanny valley proper. The LLM writes perfectly functional code that does almost what you asked for. But it doesn't have the weird decisions you'd have made at 3am. It doesn't have the "sod it, that'll do" shortcuts. It doesn't have the bit where you got bored halfway through and changed direction.

The nostalgia still hits though, when I open the page and see that grey window frame. My brain fills in the gaps because it wants to. The LLM gives you the skeleton and your memory does the rest.

For better or worse

I don't think this is a bad thing. The old web was crooked. Nothing lined up. Nothing was consistent. Half of it was broken and the other half was about to be. What I've ended up with is something new that rhymes with something old. Not a faithful recreation. More like humming a tune you half-remember and filling in the bits you've forgotten with whatever feels right.

Besides, if I'm being honest, the original were nowt special either. I just happened to be seventeen when I saw it.
